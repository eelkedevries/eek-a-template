# Writing style guide

Apply the following style guide faithfully to all written output. Read it fully before writing or editing.

---

## Core principles

Write in formal, precise, objective British English. Prioritise clarity, conceptual precision, logical structure, and careful claim calibration. The prose should feel disciplined, credible, and mildly dry — not promotional, inflated, sentimental, decorative, or overly idiomatic.

---

## Register-specific guidance

### Academic and scientific writing

Be question-driven and distinction-led. Frame the text around a specific unresolved issue rather than a broad topic. Where useful, make competing interpretations explicit and explain what would distinguish them. Move efficiently from the theoretical problem to the concrete test, criterion, example, or implication that bears on it.

Use stable terminology throughout rather than varying wording for stylistic effect. State the strongest claim justified by the evidence, while separating clearly between what is shown, what is inferred, and what remains uncertain. Use limitations to define scope and indicate what further evidence would resolve the issue.

Typical structure: theoretical framing and prior evidence → gap → present question → method or analytic logic → findings → interpretation → limitations → implications.

### Professional and technical writing

Be concise, direct, competent, and low-drama. The tone should be polite but not overly warm. Prefer clear, natural professional English consistent with a non-native speaker with high academic proficiency.

### Explanatory and essayistic writing

Where the register permits, allow a restrained Asimov-like influence: plain, intelligent, minimally ornate, logically progressive, and mildly dry. Let the interest come from the idea, not from verbal display.

---

## What to avoid

### Avoiding AI-typical prose

Do not use:
- Generic scene-setting or inflated importance language
- Empty balance or superficial synthesis
- Polished filler that says little
- Stock formulas, repetitive abstract nouns, vague claims of complexity or significance
- Transitions that merely sound smooth without advancing the argument
- Symmetrical "on the one hand … on the other hand" framing when the issue is not genuinely balanced
- Formulaic conclusions that simply restate importance

Prefer specificity over generality, unevenness over artificial smoothness, and genuine authorial judgement over comprehensive-sounding but impersonal summary. The writing should feel deliberate, selective, and text-driven — not templated, suspiciously polished, or mechanically exhaustive.

### Avoiding machine-generated formatting habits

Do not overuse boldface, em dashes, stacked contrast formulas, colon-heavy emphasis, or visually over-signposted prose. Use emphasis sparingly, and only where it genuinely improves readability.

---

## Sentence and paragraph craft

Sentences should be clear, purposeful, and grammatically controlled. Prefer explicit logical transitions, minimal redundancy, and consistent tense.

- Use **present tense** for accepted knowledge and interpretation.
- Use **past tense** for specific procedures and findings.

---

## Titles and headings

Use sentence case: capitalise only the first word, except where later capitals are required by proper nouns, acronyms, or other genuinely case-sensitive terms.

---

## Structure for longer texts

A typical progression: scope or question → relevant distinctions or background → analysis → limitations → implications.

For scientific writing: theoretical framing and prior evidence → gap → present question → method or analytic logic → findings → interpretation → limitations → implications.

---

## Summary reminder

The style should be precise, structured, restrained, and analytically explicit, with wording chosen to clarify rather than impress.

---

## Scope: prose, not prompts

This guide governs prose for human readers. It does not apply mechanically to prompts written for LLM agents, which are optimised for execution: favour unambiguous structure, complete context, explicit constraints, acceptance criteria, and test instructions over ordinary prose elegance. For a prompt, clarity to the agent outranks stylistic restraint.
